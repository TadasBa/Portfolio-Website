In 2021, after finishing school, I enrolled in the Software Engineering program at Vilnius University. This marked a completely new chapter in my life, and naturally, it felt both exciting and a bit overwhelming at first.

Now that I have graduated, I wanted to take a moment to reflect on these past four years. Writing this also feels like a good way to recap the journey for myself, and maybe share a piece of it with anyone curious or looking for some insight.

## First year

During the first year, my focus was mostly on studying. The courses were challenging and full of new concepts I had not encountered before. Besides the content itself, university was a completely different thing compared to school. The hardest part was not just the material. It was learning how to learn all over again.

Back in school, things felt easier. Teachers were usually helpful and genuinely passionate about teaching. University was different. I slowly realized that university lecturers are often not teachers in the same sense, but specialists deeply involved in their fields. That did not click for me right away.

Lectures were not always as engaging as school lessons, and the responsibility to master the material was on me. No sugarcoating: it was sink or swim.

One course that stuck with me from the first year was Computer Architecture. It was one of the toughest classes for many students and had surprisingly little online material to help, so you really had to pay attention in lectures and study the official resources carefully.

On a lighter note, another memorable course was Procedural Programming. It was not too difficult, but it had one of the best lecturers at Vilnius University, Irmantas Radavičius. His lectures were modern, passionate, interesting, and genuinely useful. That made learning a lot more enjoyable.

## Second year

The second year went by pretty fast and, honestly, was not the most memorable. By then, I had already gotten the hang of studying. The "how to university" part clicked, and I started enjoying the process more.

Concepts from different courses started to connect, which made it feel like the hardest part was behind me. Most of the courses were manageable. Not "Netflix-and-pass" easy, but doable with effort.

A few subjects that really stayed with me were Database Management Systems, Functional Programming, and Networking. They were completely new territory for me, but once I put in the effort, they turned out to be genuinely interesting.

That year I also started exploring what Vilnius University had to offer outside the classroom. One of the things I found was the student association. More specifically, I became a mentor for first-year students, which included helping organize and volunteer during the freshman camp.

It was one of the best experiences of my university journey. I met a lot of new people, made good memories, and had an absurd amount of fun, even while pretending to be a responsible adult in front of freshmen.

## Third year

Before I knew it, the third year rolled in. I felt more confident, comfortable, and like I belonged. Studies were going smoothly, and I was in my element.

One standout course that year was UX/UI Design. It involved quite a bit of theory, but it was worth the time. It highlighted that we build software not for compilers, but for people.

Then came one of the biggest highlights of my studies: Erasmus. In the second semester of third year, I spent a semester abroad at the Athens University of Economics and Business, which had its own informatics faculty.

That whole experience probably deserves its own blog post, but to keep it short, it was an unforgettable part of my academic journey.

In Athens, I took courses including Blockchain Technologies and Computer Graphics. They were not as intense as the courses back home in Vilnius, but the Greek lecturers surprised me in a good way.

Unlike what I had gotten used to, they acted more like traditional teachers: asking questions during lectures, encouraging discussions, and explaining things more than once if needed. It felt refreshingly interactive.

## Final year

And then came the final year. Cue the dramatic music.

With it came the realization: this is it. The finish line. The first semester included our last actual lectures, and two major courses stood out: Project Management and Project Quality. They were useful, but the real boss fight was the thesis coursework.

Thankfully, I got lucky. I landed a topic I was genuinely interested in, had a great supervisor, and managed to write the paper on time without spiraling into too much stress. It turned out to be a valuable project that I continued as my bachelor’s thesis.

The final semester flew by like a speeding deadline. All coursework was over, and I was fully immersed in a full-time internship and writing my bachelor’s thesis.

The topic was about large language models and, more specifically, code quality evaluation using large language models. It was not the easiest stretch, but I pushed through and completed it. I got a solid grade, and more importantly, I felt genuinely good about the work.

## Closing

So that is basically it.

I did not go too technical on purpose. This was not meant to be a course catalog or a tech deep dive. I wanted to share how I felt about the studies and the experience of being a software engineering student at Vilnius University.

If you are reading this and you are on a similar path, or considering one, I hope it gives you at least a small glimpse of what it can be like.
